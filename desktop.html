About the Estampie

Medieval instrumental dance • 13th–14th century • secular

The estampie is one of the earliest surviving purely instrumental forms of Western music — a lively courtly dance with no words, built to be played and danced rather than sung. Alongside its cousins the saltarello and ductia, it was struck up on vielle, psaltery, hurdy-gurdy and bagpipes wherever a court took to the floor.

Its form is a chain of repeated puncta: each phrase is played twice, first with an open (ouvert) ending that hangs unresolved, then with a closed (clos) ending that lands home. The few that were written down survive in the Robertsbridge Codex and the Manuscrit du Roi. This engine recreates the instruments themselves with physical modelling: Karplus–Strong waveguide strings over a droning open fifth.

Where it sits in the lineage

The estampie sits on the secular, instrumental branch of early music. It grew out of the sung monophony of the troubadours and trouvères — when their melodies stepped off the page and onto the dance floor — and so it stands apart from the sacred chant→polyphony line that runs through organum and the Ars Nova.
